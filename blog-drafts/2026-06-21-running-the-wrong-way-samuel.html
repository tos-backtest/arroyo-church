In 1964, Vikings defensive lineman Jim Marshall scooped up a fumble and sprinted 66 yards for a touchdown. The crowd roared — except they were the other team's fans. Marshall had run the wrong way and scored for the opposition. He was fast. He was sincere. He was also completely wrong.

That image frames our latest message in the Walk Faithfully series through the life of Samuel. We live in a culture that says it doesn't matter what you believe or how you live, so long as you're sincere. But sincerity isn't a compass. You can run quickly, confidently, and sincerely in the wrong direction — and if you keep it up long enough, it ends in a wreck. That's true in marriage, in our careers, and most of all in our relationship with God.

In 1 Samuel 8, Israel had just been spiritually revived under Samuel's leadership. But almost immediately they slid back to square one. Three truths from this passage show us how we run the wrong way, how to stop, and what to do when we inevitably fall.

1. Running the Wrong Way Starts by Rejecting the King

The trouble began with a request that sounded reasonable:

"Then all the elders of Israel gathered together and came to Samuel at Ramah. They said to him, 'You are old, and your sons do not follow your ways; now appoint a king to lead us, such as all the other nations have.' But when they said, 'Give us a king to lead us,' this displeased Samuel; so he prayed to the LORD. And the LORD told him: 'Listen to all that the people are saying to you; it is not you they have rejected, but they have rejected me as their king.'" (1 Samuel 8:4–7)

Israel wasn't just reorganizing their government. They were saying, in effect, "We want to be like everybody else." God had been their King, their protector, their provider. Now they wanted someone visible and ordinary, like the surrounding nations.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: every one of us has done the same thing. Each human heart has a throne, and there's only room for one occupant. So the question is simple but searching — who is sitting on the throne of your heart?

Whatever has the final say in your life is your real king. You can say Christ is King while something else actually rules. When God's Word says one thing but the world says another, which wins? When your feelings collide with God's clear instruction, which do you obey? In our part of the world, the two most common rivals to Christ's throne are money and sexuality — but the list of substitute kings is endless.

To follow Jesus is to live counter-culturally — to swim upstream against a powerful current. If you want to keep running the wrong way, just keep something other than Christ on the throne. If you want to turn around, hand Him the crown.

2. We Run the Wrong Way When We Ignore God's Warnings

God didn't let Israel walk into disaster blindly. He told Samuel to warn them plainly:

"He will take the best of your fields and vineyards and olive groves and give them to his attendants... You yourselves will become his slaves. When that day comes, you will cry out for relief from the king you have chosen, but the LORD will not answer you in that day." (1 Samuel 8, paraphrased from vv. 11–18)

They heard every word — and waved it off. "No," they said, "we want a king over us" (1 Samuel 8:19). God's warning wasn't meant to restrict them; it was meant to free them. A warning light on your dashboard isn't your enemy. Ignore it long enough and the engine blows.

God still warns His people today, usually through four channels:

  • His Word — Scripture lays out His good plan and flags the dangers ahead.
  • His people — fellow believers who lovingly say, "Be careful here."
  • His Spirit — that inner check that says, "You know where this leads."
  • The failures of others — sobering examples that warn us off the same cliff.

The choice is never whether we'll learn, only how. We can learn the easy way by listening, or the hard way by consequences. Stunningly, when Israel kept refusing, God said, "Listen to them and give them a king" (1 Samuel 8:22). Like a loving father who finally lets a stubborn child feel a small burn, God sometimes allows us to learn the painful way — not because He's angry, but because He loves us too much to leave us deaf to Him.

3. How to Respond After You've Run the Wrong Way

By 1 Samuel 12, the people realize their mistake. Samuel's response in his farewell address gives us four steps for anyone who has wandered.

Acknowledge your sin

"The people all said to Samuel, 'Pray to the LORD your God for your servants so that we will not die, for we have added to all our other sins the evil of asking for a king.'" (1 Samuel 12:19)

There is no healing without revealing. Stop deflecting and be honest — with yourself, with God, and with others. That honesty is where the turnaround begins.

Commit to serving the Lord going forward

"Do not be afraid... Yet do not turn away from the LORD, but serve the LORD with all your heart. Do not turn away after useless idols. They can do you no good, nor can they rescue you, because they are useless." (1 Samuel 12:20–21)

You can't change the past, but you can change your direction. Remember Jim Marshall? He ran the wrong way early — then went on to a Hall-of-Fame career and a record for never missing a game. Paul put it this way: "Forgetting what is behind and straining toward what is ahead" (Philippians 3:13). A man who once persecuted the church became its greatest missionary. Your past does not get to define you.

Realize God will not reject you

"For the sake of his great name the LORD will not reject his people, because the LORD was pleased to make you his own." (1 Samuel 12:22)

God's acceptance never rested on Israel's goodness, and yours never rests on yours. It rests on His great name. When you are not faithful, He is still faithful. "For the joy set before him" (Hebrews 12:2), Jesus endured the cross, bearing the weight of your sin — because He was pleased to make you His own. Don't hide like Adam and Eve. Come into the open and into His arms.

Give prayer and care to others who run

"As for me, far be it from me that I should sin against the LORD by failing to pray for you. And I will teach you the way that is good and right." (1 Samuel 12:23)

Samuel could have said, "I told you so." Instead he committed to pray for and teach the very people who ignored him. True transformation shows up not only in how we relate to God, but in how we treat people who have wronged us.

A Closing Takeaway

Proverbs 24:16 says, "Though the righteous fall seven times, they rise again." The difference between following Christ and not isn't sinlessness — it's getting back up. And we don't rise by our own strength; the risen Jesus extends His hand to lift us when we couldn't lift ourselves.

Reflection question: Who is actually on the throne of your heart this week — and is there a warning God has been lovingly repeating that you've been ignoring?
